## Applied Illustration: Separating Safe Adjustment from Time-Varying Controls in Jensen (2025)

Jensen (2025) provides a useful applied illustration of the distinction between safe adjustment and potentially contaminated time-varying controls in a non-staggered difference-in-differences design. The paper studies the effect of attending college between 2004 and 2008 on subsequent political participation using a two-period panel with individual fixed effects. In the baseline specification, the identifying variation comes from the interaction between college attendance and the post period, while the model also includes an interaction between prior voting and the post indicator. The paper then compares this baseline to specifications that add a rich set of time-varying controls and, in some specifications, interactions between the post indicator and pre-treatment covariates.

Our replication in [jensen_ivb_report.pdf](/Users/manoelgaldino/Documents/DCP/Papers/IVB/IVB-paper/replication/jensen_ivb_report.pdf) reproduces the published estimates in Table 1 very closely. In the full sample, the treatment coefficient falls from 0.137 in the baseline model to 0.130 when the time-varying controls are added and to 0.107 in the fullest specification. In the restricted-control-group sample, the corresponding estimates are 0.096, 0.106, and 0.089. On its face, this comparison suggests that the additional controls matter, but it does not reveal whether the change comes from controlling for realized post-treatment variables or from allowing for richer post-period heterogeneity using information fixed before treatment.

To separate these two operations, we estimate an intermediate specification that includes the post indicator interacted with pre-treatment covariates, but excludes the realized time-varying controls. This specification is not reported in the article, but it is the natural analogue of a safe-adjustment model in our framework. The results are informative. In the full sample, adding these pre-treatment-by-time interactions moves the coefficient from 0.137 to 0.111, while moving from this safe-adjustment model to the full model with time-varying controls changes the estimate only from 0.111 to 0.107. In the restricted-control-group sample, the safe-adjustment model lowers the coefficient from 0.096 to 0.078, whereas the full model with time-varying controls yields 0.089.

The substantive implication is that Jensen (2025) is not best understood as a case in which post-treatment controls alone drive the result. Rather, the main movement comes from richer adjustment for differential post-period dynamics using pre-treatment information. Once that safe-adjustment block is separated out, the incremental contribution of the realized time-varying controls is more modest and, in the restricted sample, even points in a different direction than the safe adjustment. This is precisely the distinction our framework is meant to clarify. The applied question is not simply whether one should include the post-period controls, but whether the adjustment that makes the identifying assumption more plausible can be achieved using pre-treatment information alone.

For our purposes, Jensen (2025) is therefore a stronger application than Kronick (2020). Kronick already arrives at a substantively appropriate adjustment strategy, so our framework mainly clarifies interpretation. In Jensen, by contrast, the framework changes the way one reads the specification table itself: what appears to be a comparison between “with” and “without” time-varying controls partly masks the role of a safer adjustment using pre-treatment covariates interacted with time.
