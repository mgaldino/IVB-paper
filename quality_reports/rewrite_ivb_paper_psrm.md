# Introduction

Every researcher who includes a time-varying covariate in a panel regression is making a bet: that the variable removes more confounding than it introduces in distortion. The bet is often wrong. If the covariate is a collider---caused by both the treatment and the outcome---including it opens a spurious path and biases the estimate. If it is a contemporaneous mediator, including it blocks the indirect causal channel and attenuates the total effect. And in TSCS data, the same variable can switch roles depending on when it is measured: GDP per capita at $t-1$ may be a confounder that the researcher needs; GDP per capita at $t$ may be a collider or mediator that the researcher should avoid. Standard heuristics for control selection---include causes of the outcome, or common causes of treatment and outcome---do not distinguish these cases \citep{cinelli2021crash}.

The problem is recognized on both sides of a disciplinary divide that has produced parallel but disconnected responses. In the DID literature, \citet{caetano_callaway2024} and \citet{lin_zhang2022} have shown that time-varying covariates create specification problems even under parallel trends, and the ``bad controls'' problem in DID is the subject of active research \citep{caetano_etal2022}. In the TSCS literature, \citet{blackwell2018make} and \citet{imai_kim2021} have recommended ADL models that condition on lagged states. Neither tradition offers an estimable formula that tells the applied researcher how much a specific covariate inclusion changes the treatment estimate.

This paper provides such a formula. The Included Variable Bias (IVB) diagnostic repurposes the standard short-versus-long regression decomposition:
\begin{equation}
\text{IVB} = -\theta^{\star} \times \pi
\label{eq:ivb_main}
\end{equation}
where $\theta^{\star}$ is the coefficient on the covariate in the augmented regression and $\pi$ is the coefficient on the treatment in an auxiliary regression of the covariate on the treatment and legitimate controls.^[In the cross-sectional derivation (Section~\ref{sec:cs_derivation}), $\theta^{\star}$ corresponds to $\beta_2^{\star}$ and $\pi$ corresponds to $\phi_1$; the cross-sectional notation appears in that section and its comparison table, while we use the TSCS notation ($\theta^{\star}$, $\pi$) in all subsequent discussion.] Both components come from regressions the researcher has already run or can run immediately. The formula mirrors the Omitted Variable Bias formula but works in the opposite direction: where OVB measures the cost of leaving out a confounder, IVB measures the change from putting in an additional variable. The algebra is an identity of OLS via the Frisch--Waugh--Lovell theorem \citep{frisch1933partial, lovell1963seasonal}; it holds in cross-sectional, TWFE, and ADL models for any treatment type. What makes it a diagnostic, not just a decomposition, is the DAG: the graph tells the researcher whether the change is collider bias, omitted variable bias, or over-control of a mediator.

The formula is the first of two tools. The second is a benchmark specification. For the contemporaneous treatment effect (CET) in TSCS settings, ADL + FE with lagged state variables avoids the problems that contemporaneous covariates create. The lagged value $Z_{t-1}$ absorbs confounding without opening a collider path or blocking the mediator channel $D_t \to Z_t \to Y_t$. This timing logic exploits a structural advantage of TSCS data over canonical two-period DID, where the researcher cannot separate the confounding role of $Z$ from its post-treatment role. Monte Carlo simulations across 238 DGP configurations---spanning collider, dual-role, and mediator cases with nonlinearities and outcome-to-treatment feedback---confirm that the ADL benchmark keeps bias below 3\% of the treatment effect.

We apply the diagnostic to six published studies in comparative politics and international political economy. For each study, we classify every control variable using DAG-based reasoning grounded in the published literature. Among 14 theoretically motivated collider candidates, the median $|\text{IVB}/\text{SE}|$ is approximately 0.13---about one-eighth of a standard error. Only GDP per capita in a growth regression \citep{rogowski_etal2022} exceeds one standard error, and its collider status is causally ambiguous because it is simultaneously a confounder. The typical finding is that the specification choice does not matter much. Knowing that is itself the point: the formula lets the researcher verify, rather than assume, that including or excluding a covariate is quantitatively inconsequential.
