  Benchmark Specification for the Contemporaneous Treatment

                                                      Abstract

       We study a practical problem in time-series cross-sectional (TSCS) research: whether observed time-

   varying covariates should be included as controls when the estimand is the contemporaneous treatment

   effect (CET). The same covariate can function as a confounder, a collider, or a mediator depending on

   its timing relative to the treatment, and choosing wrong can introduce bias rather than remove it. We

   present the Included Variable Bias (IVB) formula—IVB = −θ⋆ × π—a closed-form diagnostic, based on

   the well-known short-versus-long regression decomposition, that quantifies the change in the treatment

   estimate caused by including or excluding a covariate, using quantities the researcher already has. The

   formula mirrors the classic Omitted Variable Bias formula but addresses the opposite problem; com-

   bined with a Directed Acyclic Graph, it tells the researcher whether the change represents collider bias,

   omitted variable bias, or over-control of a mediator. We show that in TSCS settings, a benchmark ADL

   + FE specification with lagged state variables avoids conditioning on contemporaneous post-treatment

   covariates: it blocks collider paths, preserves the total contemporaneous effect when covariates are lagged

   rather than contemporaneous, and absorbs confounding through lagged values. Monte Carlo simulations

   covering collider, dual-role, and mediator cases across 238 DGP configurations confirm that this bench-

   mark keeps bias below 3% of the treatment effect. We apply the diagnostic to six published studies

   spanning democratization, peacekeeping, fiscal capacity, infrastructure, and sovereign debt, classifying

   each control via DAG-based reasoning. Among 14 theoretically motivated collider candidates, the median

   IVB is approximately 0.13 standard errors—well within sampling noise.

1     Introduction

Researchers using time-series cross-sectional data constantly face a deceptively simple specification choice:

should an observed time-varying covariate be included in the regression? Consider an international-relations

scholar studying the effect of UN peacekeeping on democratization. Conditioning on conflict intensity is

tempting because more severe conflicts are more likely to receive peacekeeping missions and are less likely to

democratize. But peacekeeping can itself reduce violence. If so, conditioning on contemporaneous conflict

intensity means conditioning on a variable that may already have responded to treatment. Omitting conflict

may leave confounding; including realized conflict may introduce a different problem. The same logic appears

whenever researchers want to control for GDP, aid, inflation, turnout, repression, or any other variable that

evolves within the same period as treatment and outcome.

Recent work on DID with time-varying covariates makes this problem stark. When a covariate can itself

respond to treatment, both conditioning on the realized covariate and omitting it can be problematic,

because the causally relevant object may be the untreated potential covariate Zt (0) rather than the realized

Zt (Caetano and Callaway, 2024; Caetano et al., 2022; Lin and Zhang, 2022). Related work on panels and

causal dynamics likewise shows that time-varying covariates can create demanding identification problems

(Blackwell and Glynn, 2018; Imai and Kim, 2021). We take that broader literature as the starting point

rather than the target of critique.

Our contribution is different and narrower, but also more operational. We introduce included variable bias

(IVB) as a general diagnostic problem. In linear nested models, the resulting specification shift admits a

simple closed-form decomposition that repurposes the standard short-versus-long regression decomposition:


                                                       IVB = −θ⋆ × π                                                         (1)


where θ⋆ is the coefficient on the covariate in the augmented regression and π is the coefficient on the

treatment in an auxiliary regression of the covariate on the treatment and legitimate controls.1 Both com-

ponents come from regressions the researcher has already run or can run immediately. The formula mirrors

the Omitted Variable Bias formula but works in the opposite direction: where OVB measures the cost of

leaving out a confounder, IVB measures the change from putting in an additional variable. The algebra is

an identity of OLS via the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem (Frisch and Waugh, 1933; Lovell, 1963); it holds

in cross-sectional, TWFE, and ADL models for any treatment type. What makes it a diagnostic, not just

The formula is the first of two tools. The second is a benchmark specification. For the contemporaneous

treatment effect (CET) in TSCS settings, ADL + FE with lagged state variables avoids the problems

that contemporaneous covariates create. The lagged value Zt−1 absorbs confounding without opening a

collider path or blocking the mediator channel Dt → Zt → Yt . Monte Carlo simulations across 238 DGP

configurations confirm that the ADL benchmark keeps bias below 3% of the treatment effect. The same

IVB logic also applies to DiD settings where researchers include time-varying controls to satisfy conditional

parallel trends: we show how to decompose the specification shift into a safe pre-treatment adjustment and

the marginal cost of adding realized post-treatment controls.

We apply the diagnostic to six published studies in comparative politics and international political economy.

For each study, we classify every control variable using DAG-based reasoning grounded in the published

literature. Among 14 theoretically motivated collider candidates, the median |IVB/SE| is approximately

0.13—about one-eighth of a standard error. Only GDP per capita in a growth regression (Rogowski et al.,

2022) exceeds one standard error, and its collider status is causally ambiguous because it is simultaneously a

confounder. The typical finding is that the specification choice does not matter much. Knowing that is itself

the point: the formula lets the researcher verify, rather than assume, that including or excluding a covariate

is quantitatively inconsequential.

This architecture helps distinguish the paper from two nearby contributions. It is not only a warning that

time-varying controls are hard, because IVB gives that difficulty a named and estimable diagnostic. And

it is not only a recommendation to use dynamic specifications, because the benchmark is presented as the

main applied implication of a more general included-variable-bias framework.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~2 defines the control-variable problem when

covariates may respond to treatment. Section~3 uses DAGs to show how timing determines a covariate’s

causal role. Section~4 presents the IVB formula for cross-sectional, TWFE, and ADL models. Section~5

uses Monte Carlo simulations to benchmark dynamic specifications for the CET. Section~6 extends the

decomposition to DiD with time-varying controls, using Jensen (2025) as a proof of concept. Section~7

applies the formula to six published TSCS studies. Section~8 concludes.

2      The Time-Varying Control Problem for the Contemporaneous

       Treatment Effect

Applied researchers working with TSCS data rarely face a binary choice between control'' anddo not

control.’ ’ The more realistic problem is whether an observed time-varying covariate should enter the spec-

ification when the estimand is the contemporaneous treatment effect (CET). The same variable can help,

hurt, or redefine the estimand depending on timing. A contemporaneous covariate Zt may be a confounder,

a collider, or a mediator. Its lag, Zt−1 , may instead behave as a predetermined state variable relative to the

CET.

Standard heuristics are too coarse for this problem. Control checking'' recommends adding predictors

of the outcome to gain precision;confounding checking’ ’ recommends adding joint causes of treatment

and outcome (Cinelli, Forney and Pearl, 2022). Both ignore the possibility that a candidate control is post-

treatment in the period of interest. That omission is costly in TSCS designs because treatment, outcome,

and covariates evolve jointly over time, often with persistence in all three processes (Blackwell and Glynn,

2018; Imai and Kim, 2021). Recent DID work sharpens the problem further: if Zt may itself respond

to treatment, neither conditioning on realized Zt nor omitting it necessarily identifies the target estimand

(Caetano and Callaway, 2024; Caetano et al., 2022). Even formal approaches leave a quantification gap:

the DAG framework tells researchers whether a variable is a collider but not how much bias results from

conditioning on it.2

Our answer has two parts. The general contribution is IVB: a named diagnostic that expresses the change

from including a covariate in terms of regression coefficients the researcher already has (θ̂⋆ from the long

regression, π̂ from an auxiliary regression)—analogous to how Cinelli and Hazlett (2020) recast OVB sensi-

tivity in terms of partial R2 . Combined with a DAG, the formula tells the researcher whether the difference

represents collider bias, omitted variable bias, or over-control. The more specific contribution is the pa-

per’s payoff in TSCS settings with the CET: a dynamic benchmark based on ADL + FE with lagged state

variables.

The scope qualification matters. A lagged control is pre-treatment only relative to the effect of Dt on Yt .

That does not make it universally innocuous for cumulative, delayed, or long-run effects. The paper’s positive

recommendation is therefore explicitly local to the CET.

3     DAGs, Timing, and Causal Roles

We assume familiarity with directed acyclic graphs and their three elementary structures—forks, chains, and

colliders (see Pearl, 2009; Cinelli, Forney and Pearl, 2022, for introductions). The key fact for what follows

is that conditioning on a collider—a common effect of treatment and outcome—creates rather than removes

a spurious association between its causes (Elwert and Winship, 2014). We now illustrate this in a TSCS

context.


3.1    A TSCS Application: Political Change and Civil War

Consider a typical setup in comparative politics. A researcher wants to estimate the causal effect of political

regime change on civil war, as in Hegre et al. (2001). Suppose the only measured confounder is per capita

income, which plausibly causes both political change and civil war (Blattman and Miguel, 2010). The

researcher controls for per capita income to block the backdoor path.

In a TSCS setting, variables must be indexed by time. The researcher decides to lag both per capita income

and political change to address reverse causality concerns. The resulting model corresponds to the temporal

DAG in Figure~1.

A key insight from this DAG is that the dynamic structure introduces backdoor paths through the lagged

outcome (CWt ). To block these paths, the researcher must include CWt as a control—leading to a dynamic

panel (ADL) specification. Without the lagged dependent variable, the causal effect is biased.

We use the term foreign collider bias descriptively, not analytically—it denotes the practical diffi-

culty that the causal knowledge needed to identify a collider often resides in literatures outside the

researcher’s primary domain. In this example, discovering that Democracy Level is a collider requires

consulting   the     foreign'' literature on the determinants of democracy, which is typically

separate from the civil war literature motivating the original study. The phenomenon

is not limited to economic variables: in studies of voter turnout ($Y$) and campaign

spending ($D$), a researcher controlling for media coverage ($Z$) faces the same pattern

if both spending and turnout causally affect media coverage---a relationship documented

in the communications literature rather than the electoral behavior literature. The

standard heuristics for including controls review the literature on the \textit{outcome}

and the \textit{treatment}, but they do not instruct the researcher to investigate whether

the candidate control is itself caused by the treatment or outcome through an unobserved

variable. The underlying causal phenomenon is standard collider bias; whatforeign’ ’ adds is a



                                                     6
practical warning about the disciplinary boundaries that make such colliders easy to miss.

The concept of foreign collider bias has broad applicability in TSCS research.           Consider a researcher

studying the effect of democratization on ethnic inequality who controls for GDP per capita (Leipziger,

2024).         The domestic'' literature on ethnic inequality identifies GDP as a correlate of

inequality and a plausible confounder. But theforeign’ ’ literature on economic growth establishes

that democracy causes GDP growth (Acemoglu et al., 2019), and that ethnic inequality causally reduces

GDP (Grundler and Link, 2024). These two causal channels—D → Z and Y → Z—make GDP per capita

a collider as well as a confounder. Discovering this requires consulting the growth literature, not just the

inequality literature. Similarly, in studies of public infrastructure and economic growth (Rogowski et al.,

2022), GDP per capita is simultaneously the confounding variable that growth regressions must control for

(conditional convergence) and a collider through the paper’s core mechanism (D → Z). This pattern is

particularly easy to miss because researchers following best practices—reviewing the literature on potential

confounders—may not think to investigate the determinants of each candidate control variable in adjacent

literatures.

This example illustrates the power of DAGs: by making the causal structure explicit, they reveal that

Democracy Level is a collider and that conditioning on it biases the regression. But the DAG alone does not

tell us how much bias results. For that, we need a formula.


3.3      Timing Determines Causal Role

The civil war example illustrates collider bias, but the same variable can play different causal roles depending

on its temporal index. This is the central complication of control variable selection in TSCS settings: the

researcher must decide not only which variables to include, but when they are measured. We distinguish

three cases that are central to the analysis that follows.

Collider. When both the treatment and the outcome (or an unobserved common cause of the outcome)

causally affect Z within the same period—Dt → Zt ← Yt —conditioning on Zt opens a spurious path. This

is the foreign collider bias case illustrated above.

Contemporaneous mediator. When the treatment affects Z and Z in turn affects the outcome within

the same period—Dt → Zt → Yt —the variable Zt is a mediator. Conditioning on Zt blocks the indirect

causal channel Dt → Zt → Yt , so the regression recovers only the direct effect of Dt on Yt . If the researcher’s

estimand is the total contemporaneous effect (CET), including Zt introduces over-control bias: the estimate

is attenuated by exactly the indirect effect that Zt mediates.



                                                       7
Dual role across time. In dynamic settings, the same variable often plays both roles across different time

indices. The lagged value Zt−1 may be a confounder—affecting both Dt and Yt through persistence—while

the contemporaneous value Zt is a collider or mediator. This dual role creates a tension: excluding Z

entirely leaves confounding from Zt−1 unaddressed, while including Zt introduces collider or mediator bias.

The resolution is to condition on Zt−1 but not on Zt : the lagged value absorbs the confounding channel

without opening the contemporaneous collider path or blocking the contemporaneous mediator path. This is

precisely the logic behind the ADL specification with lagged state variables that we benchmark in Section~5.

The IVB formula developed in the next section quantifies the arithmetic difference between specifications that

include and exclude a covariate. Whether that difference represents collider bias, over-control, or appropriate

just a qualitative classification—making the specification choice transparent and auditable.



4       The Included Variable Bias Diagnostic

The paper’s general contribution is to define included variable bias as a distinct diagnostic problem. Once

researchers accept that an observed control may itself respond to treatment, they need a language for the

resulting specification shift. DAGs classify the control; IVB quantifies what that classification means for the

treatment coefficient. The decomposition is a direct application of the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem (see,

e.g., Angrist and Pischke, 2009, Theorem 3.2.1). Our contribution is not the algebra but its repackaging

as an operational diagnostic for TSCS settings. We begin with the cross-sectional case and then extend to

ADL models.

Proposition~1 is an algebraic identity—the complement of the standard OVB decomposition, well known in

econometrics (e.g., Goldberger, 1991, pp. 197–198). Its value as a contribution lies not in the algebra but

in its application. Prior work on collider bias quantification—including path-tracing formulas (Pearl, 2013),

structural equation model derivations (Greenland, 2003), and the M-bias sensitivity analysis of Ding and

Miratrix (2015)—parameterizes the bias in terms of structural coefficients of the underlying data generating

process, which are not directly observable by the applied researcher. The IVB formula repurposes the known

decomposition as a practical diagnostic by expressing the same quantity in terms of regression coefficients

the researcher already has in hand (θ̂⋆ from the augmented regression, π̂ from a simple auxiliary regression).

This makes the formula (i)~immediately operational as a diagnostic tool, (ii)~extensible to the ADL models

prevalent in TSCS research (Propositions~2–4), and (iii)~a natural mirror of the OVB formula that applied

researchers already use routinely.3

The key difference lies in the direction of the problem. With OVB, the researcher runs a short regression

when the long regression is correct, and the bias equals the product of two regression coefficients involving the

omitted variable. With IVB, the researcher runs a long regression when the short regression is correct, and

the bias is the negative product of two analogous coefficients involving the included collider. The negative sign

in the IVB formula arises because the mechanisms operate in opposite directions: OVB captures the effect

of an omitted common cause channeled through the treatment-outcome relationship, while IVB captures the

spurious association created by conditioning on a common effect.

A notable practical advantage of IVB is that its components are always estimable: the researcher has already

included Z in the regression, so β2⋆ is available, and ϕ1 comes from a simple auxiliary regression. By contrast,

OVB requires observing the omitted variable—which, by definition, is often unavailable.

   where πj is the coefficient on Dt−j in the auxiliary regression Zt = a + πj Dt−j + Wt′ δ + ηt .

   The practical value of this decomposition lies in its uniformity across specifications. Regardless of the number

   of lags in the outcome or the treatment, the IVB for any coefficient βj is always the negative product of two

   estimable quantities: the collider coefficient θ⋆ in the long model, and the partial association πj between the

   treatment at lag j and the collider, controlling for all other legitimate regressors.

   An important practical observation is that while the form of the IVB is simple, the magnitude can vary

   substantially depending on the dynamics. When the treatment Dt is highly predictable from lags, the

   residualized treatment D̃t has little variation, and π can become unstable. When Zt is a post-treatment

   variable that responds to y or to correlated shocks, both θ⋆ ̸= 0 and π ̸= 0 tend to arise mechanically.

   Two additional results are developed in the appendix: the interaction between IVB and Nickell bias in short

   panels (Appendix~A.3), and the fact that replacing the treatment with a longer lag does not mechanically

   eliminate the IVB when the treatment is autocorrelated (Appendix~A.4).

4.7    Interpretation Caveats

An important caveat concerns the interpretation of the IVB formula. The decomposition IVB = −θ⋆ × π is

an algebraic identity: it holds whenever the short and long regressions are nested, regardless of the causal

role of Z. The formula quantifies the arithmetic difference between two nested specifications; it does not,

by itself, determine which specification is causally correct. The product θ⋆ × π will be non-zero whenever Z

is correlated with both D and y conditional on other controls—a condition satisfied not only when Z is a



                                                      15
collider, but also when Z is a confounder or a mediator.

The three cases lead to fundamentally different conclusions:

    • Z is a collider. Then the short regression is correctly specified, the IVB formula gives the bias

      introduced by including Z, and the researcher should exclude Z.

    • Z is a confounder. Then the long regression is correctly specified, and the same algebraic decom-

      position gives the omitted variable bias in the short regression. In this case, excluding Z introduces

      bias rather than removing it. The formula is still informative—it tells the researcher how sensitive the

      treatment estimate is to including Z—but its sign is reversed: a large IVB is evidence that Z is an

      important confounder, not that it is a harmful collider.

    • Z is a contemporaneous mediator. Then Zt lies on the causal path Dt → Zt → Yt , and the

      long regression recovers the direct effect while the short regression recovers the total effect. The IVB

      formula captures the difference between the two—i.e., the indirect effect mediated by Zt . In TSCS

      settings where the estimand is the contemporaneous total effect (CET), the researcher should avoid

      conditioning on Zt and instead condition on the lagged value Zt−1 , which captures confounding without

      blocking the contemporaneous causal channel (Section 3.3).

Only the DAG determines which case applies. The IVB formula complements DAGs but cannot substitute

for them: the graph tells the researcher whether Z is a collider, and only then does the formula quantify

how much damage including it causes. We illustrate this interplay in Section~7, where we combine literature

review with DAG reasoning to assess collider plausibility before computing the IVB. The Rogowski et

al. application in particular shows that a large IVB does not automatically imply collider bias—it may

instead reflect appropriate deconfounding.

When Z is simultaneously a confounder and a collider—the empirically prevalent dual-role case—the IVB

formula still quantifies the arithmetic difference between specifications, but its causal interpretation requires

additional assumptions (Ding and Miratrix, 2015). The ADL benchmark resolves this dual-role tension by

conditioning on Zt−1 (which captures confounding) while leaving Zt out (which avoids the collider channel).



5     Benchmarking Dynamic Specifications for the CET

The IVB formula tells a researcher how much the treatment estimate changes when a covariate is included.

The DAG tells the researcher what that change means. Together, they raise a practical question: which

specification should a TSCS researcher adopt when the estimand is the contemporaneous treatment effect


                                                       16
(CET)?

We use Monte Carlo simulations spanning 238 DGP configurations and 500 replications each to evaluate

specifications across three empirically relevant scenarios: collider and dual-role controls, contemporaneous

mediators, and robustness to nonlinearity and feedback. The evidence converges on a single recommendation:

for the CET in TSCS settings, ADL + FE with lagged state variables provides a simple and defensible

benchmark.


5.1     Collider and Dual-Role Controls

The most common scenario in TSCS applications is a covariate Z that plays a dual role: Zt−1 is a con-

founder (it affects both Dt and Yt through persistence), while Zt is a collider (Dt → Zt ← Yt ). The

researcher faces a dilemma: excluding Z entirely leaves confounding from Zt−1 unaddressed, while including

the contemporaneous Zt introduces collider bias.

Two features of TWFE already attenuate the collider channel. First, as shown in Corollary~1, the IVB

under TWFE depends only on the within-unit components of θ⋆ and π. If the association between D and Z

is primarily cross-sectional (e.g., richer countries have better governance in levels), unit fixed effects absorb

this association and π is small. An analogous argument applies to θ⋆ . Second, in studies with binary, slow-

moving treatments (democratization, peacekeeping deployment, policy adoption), few “switchers’ ’ change

treatment status, inflating SE(β̂) while the population IVB remains roughly constant. In Monte Carlo

simulations (N = 200, T = 30, β = 1, 500 replications), reducing the fraction of switchers from 70% to 10%

increases the standard error by a factor of 2–3, reducing |IVB/SE| proportionally.4

But TWFE alone does not resolve the dual-role case. To quantify the gain from dynamic specifications, we

A richer case arises when Z is simultaneously a contemporaneous mediator and a confounder through lags

(Zt−1 → Dt , Zt−1 → Yt ). In this configuration, the researcher needs Z to satisfy conditional parallel

trends, but including Zt would generate over-control. The ADL with Zt−1 resolves this tension: it removes

confounding from Zt−1 (satisfying conditional parallel trends) without blocking the contemporaneous causal

channel Dt → Zt → Yt . In simulations with confounding parameters γD = 0.15, γY = 0.2: excluding Z

entirely yields bias of 6–11% of β (OVB from the confounding channel); including Zt reduces bias to 2% but

attenuates the estimate (over-control of the indirect effect); including only Zt−1 achieves both goals, with

bias of approximately 3% and no over-control of the contemporaneous channel.

This result highlights a structural advantage of the TSCS setting over the canonical two-period DID. In

DID with two periods, the researcher has only the contemporaneous Zt∗ : including it satisfies conditional

parallel trends but generates over-control; excluding it avoids over-control but violates conditional parallel

trends. This is the dilemma motivating doubly robust and imputation-based methods in the DID literature

(Caetano et al., 2022). In TSCS, the temporal structure separates the two roles of Z: the lagged value

absorbs confounding, and the contemporaneous value is left out of the regression, preserving the total effect.

5.3    Robustness

The preceding results are derived under linearity. Two departures from linearity could threaten the ADL

benchmark: nonlinearity in the collider or mediator equation, and feedback from outcomes to treatment.

Nonlinearity. We simulate three classes of nonlinearity in the Z equation: (i)~concave transformations

(log(1 + |D|), soft-clamped polynomial), (ii)~polynomial terms (D2 ), and (iii)~interaction terms (D × H

with H exogenous). For bounded nonlinearities—concave or saturating transformations that are realistic

for most political science applications—the ADL model with all lags remains robust: bias stays below 3%

of β for the collider case and over-control remains within 5% of the linear baseline for the mediator case.

For unbounded nonlinearities (e.g., high-degree polynomials with large coefficients), the DGP can become

explosive. However, such configurations are empirically implausible: they require Z to respond without

bound to marginal increases in D, which contradicts the ceiling and floor effects that characterize most

real-world variables.

Feedback. When the outcome causally affects future treatment (Yt−1 → Dt ), strict exogeneity is violated

(Imai and Kim, 2021). We simulate this by adding a feedback parameter ϕ ∈ {0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.15} to the

treatment equation. TWFE bias grows monotonically with ϕ: from 43% of β at ϕ = 0 to 80% at ϕ = 0.15.

The ADL specification with all lags remains robust: |bias| ≤ 1% of β across all stable scenarios, because

Yt−1 in the regression absorbs the feedback channel. For ϕ ≥ 0.18, the joint dynamic system becomes non-

stationary (the companion matrix of the reduced-form VAR has eigenvalues exceeding unity), so the DGP

does not generate stable time series—this is a boundary on the data-generating process itself, not on the

ADL estimator.


5.4    Practical Benchmark for the CET

The simulation evidence converges on a practical rule for TSCS researchers interested in the contemporaneous

treatment effect:

      When the estimand is the CET, use ADL + FE with lagged state variables as the benchmark

      specification. Include Zt−1 when the lagged covariate is substantively appropriate (confounder or

      dual role). Avoid conditioning on contemporaneous Zt when Zt may be post-treatment—either a

      collider or a mediator in the period of interest.

This recommendation is consistent with and reinforces the qualitative guidance of Blackwell and Glynn


                                                          20
(2018), who showed that the CET (β1 ) is consistent in the ADL under linearity (p. 1073), and of Imai and

Kim (2021), who recommend ADL + FE as the parametric solution for dynamic panel identification (Table

1, rows 2–4). Our contribution is to provide (a) a quantitative diagnostic formula for the change caused by

specification choices, and (b) systematic Monte Carlo evidence across 238 DGP configurations—spanning

collider, dual-role, and mediator cases with nonlinearities and feedback—that the ADL benchmark works

uniformly for these cases. Specifically, the rule is supported by three results. First, for collider and dual-role

controls, ADL + FE reduces bias to less than 3% of β across all 48 dual-role scenarios, compared to 77–

169% for TWFE without controls. Second, for contemporaneous mediators, conditioning on Zt−1 instead

of Zt preserves the total effect while still absorbing confounding through lags—a timing insight that, to

our knowledge, has not been systematically demonstrated in prior work. Third, the results are robust to

bounded nonlinearities and moderate outcome-to-treatment feedback.

Additional simulations sharpen the scope of this recommendation. When the outcome equation contains

nonlinear effects of Zt−1 or level-dependent trends of the kind emphasized in recent DID work (Caetano

and Callaway, 2024), the bias of the ADL benchmark never exceeds 0.4 percent of β across stable scenarios.

These are therefore not practical boundary conditions for the benchmark. By contrast, ADL does not resolve

contemporaneous unobserved confounding (Bellemare, Masaki and Pepinsky, 2017). In simulations where an

unobserved variable affects both Dt and Yt in the same period, the bias ranges from 8 to 53 percent of β, while

an oracle specification that observes the confounder keeps bias below 0.3 percent. The benchmark therefore

addresses included-variable bias and related timing problems, not classical endogeneity from unobserved

contemporaneous confounding.

The rule has explicit limits. It applies to the contemporaneous effect of Dt on Yt ; for lagged, cumulative,

or long-run effects, Zt−1 may itself be post-treatment and the timing argument does not apply. A lagged

control is pre-treatment only relative to the effect of Dt on Yt ; that does not make it universally innocuous for

cumulative, delayed, or long-run effects. The rule requires that the DAG be at least approximately known.

And it does not claim to replace more flexible estimators in settings where the ADL’s linearity assumption

is strongly violated or where the panel dimension T is too short for Nickell bias to be negligible.

6.1    The DiD Dilemma

The preceding sections developed the IVB framework for TSCS settings where the ADL benchmark resolves

the collider/mediator/confounder dilemma through temporal separation. In difference-in-differences, the

same practical problem arises but the ADL solution is not available: the researcher typically has only two

periods, so there are no lagged dependent variables to condition on. Yet the dilemma is equally pressing.

Conditional parallel trends may require adjusting for a covariate Z that evolves over time, but the realized

post-treatment value Zt may itself respond to treatment. Omitting Z risks violating parallel trends; including

the realized Zt risks post-treatment bias.

The standard recommendation is to condition on pre-treatment values of Z (Caetano et al., 2022; Abadie,

2005). We do not propose a different recommendation. What we add is an operational decomposition

that quantifies how much each adjustment layer—pre-treatment versus realized post-treatment—moves the

treatment estimate. This allows the researcher to see whether the “specification shift’ ’ between a model

without controls and a model with time-varying controls is driven primarily by the safe pre-treatment

adjustment or by the potentially contaminated realized controls.

7.1     Application 1: Democracy and Ethnic Inequality

Leipziger (2024) estimates the effect of democratic transitions on socioeconomic ethnic inequality using

TWFE:

                  Ethnic Inequalityit = β Democracyi,t−1 + θ GDP p.c.i,t−1 + δi + γt + εit


where ethnic inequality is measured by three indices (V-Dem public services, nightlight-based income from

Alesina et al., and education from Omoeva et al.), Democracy is a binary indicator based on the Lexical Index

(≥ 5), and GDP per capita is the sole time-varying control in the baseline specification. Leipziger deliberately

excludes additional controls from the baseline, citing “the risk of posttreatment bias’ ’ (p. 1347)—a concern

consistent with the collider bias framework developed in this paper.

The replicated treatment coefficient is β̂ = −0.035 (SE = 0.012, N = 15, 191), indicating that democratiza-

tion reduces ethnic inequality in public services.


7.1.1    Collider Assessment

Is GDP per capita a collider in this setting? The question requires consulting two literatures.

First, does democracy (the treatment) cause GDP per capita? The evidence is strong. Acemoglu et al. (2019)

use dynamic panel methods, system GMM, and regional democratization waves as instruments to estimate

that democratization increases GDP per capita by approximately 20% over 25 years, operating through

investment, education, and reduced social conflict. Papaioannou and Siourounis (2008) find approximately

1 percentage point of additional annual growth following democratization.

Second, does ethnic inequality (the outcome) cause GDP per capita? The evidence is also strong. Easterly

and Levine (1997) showed that ethnic diversity explains poor growth performance in Africa through political

instability and underinvestment in public goods. Grundler and Link (2024) use instrumental variables

based on the artificiality of subnational borders in Africa to estimate a causal effect: moving from an equal

distribution to complete concentration reduces GDP per capita by 12–15%.

Both channels—D → Z and Y → Z—are supported by credible causal evidence, making GDP per capita a

plausible collider. This is an instance of foreign collider bias: the researcher studying ethnic inequality must

consult the growth economics literature to discover that GDP per capita is not merely a confounder but also

a collider.

7.2     Application 2: Postal Infrastructure and Economic Growth

Rogowski et al. (2022) estimate the effect of public postal infrastructure on economic development using a

TWFE country panel model. The main specification is:


                        GDP growthi,t+1 = β Post Officesi,t−1 + Z′it γ + δi + µt + εit


where GDP growth is the 5-year growth rate (one period forward), Post Offices is the log cumulative stock

(5-year lag), and Zit includes four controls: log GDP per capita (5-year lag), log population, urbanization

The replicated treatment coefficient is β̂ = 0.0198 (SE = 0.0054, N = 1, 540). Post offices have a statistically

significant positive effect on subsequent GDP growth.


7.2.1   Collider Assessment

The key collider candidate is log GDP per capita. The paper’s core argument is that post offices facilitate

economic development (D → Z)—indeed, this is precisely the mechanism being tested. At the same time,

GDP growth (the outcome) mechanically determines future GDP per capita levels (Y → Z, through the

accounting identity that growth cumulates into levels). This creates a collider structure: both D and Y

cause Z.

However, GDP per capita is simultaneously a strong confounder in growth regressions, through conditional

convergence: richer countries grow more slowly, holding other factors constant. This is a well-established

empirical regularity in the growth literature (Acemoglu et al., 2019). Whether controlling for GDP per capita

introduces collider bias (through the D → Z and Y → Z channels) or removes confounding bias (through

the convergence channel) depends on the relative strength of these effects—a question the IVB formula alone

cannot answer.

7.2.3     The Limits of the IVB Formula

This application illustrates the crucial caveat from Section~4.7: a large IVB does not automatically

imply collider bias. The 58% IVB for GDP per capita could reflect either of two causal structures:

   • If GDP per capita is primarily a collider (because post offices raise GDP through the paper’s core

        mechanism, and growth mechanically cumulates into GDP levels), then including it biases the treatment

        effect upward, and the short model’s estimate of 0.0083 is closer to the truth.

   • If GDP per capita is primarily a confounder (because richer countries both build more post offices

        and grow more slowly due to convergence), then excluding it biases the treatment effect downward,

        and the long model’s estimate of 0.0198 is closer to the truth.

In reality, GDP per capita is likely both a confounder and a collider, and the net effect depends on the

relative magnitudes of these channels. The IVB formula cannot resolve this ambiguity—it can only quantify


                                                       30
the arithmetic difference between the two specifications. The researcher must use domain knowledge and

causal reasoning to determine which specification is more appropriate.

This case underscores that the IVB formula is a diagnostic tool, not a decision rule. It tells the researcher

exactly how much the treatment estimate changes when a covariate is included. Whether that change

represents the introduction of bias (collider), the removal of bias (confounder), or over-control of a mediator

requires a DAG. The Rogowski et al. case is precisely the dual-role scenario discussed in Section~5.1: the

researcher needs domain knowledge to determine the net causal role before deciding whether to include,

exclude, or lag the covariate.



8    Conclusion

Applied researchers using TSCS data routinely face a practical question: should a time-varying covariate

be included as a control? The answer depends on the covariate’s causal role—confounder, collider, or

mediator—and on its timing relative to the treatment. This paper provides two tools for navigating that

decision.

The first is the IVB formula, IVB = −θ⋆ ×π, which quantifies the arithmetic difference between specifications

that include and exclude a covariate. Both components are directly estimable from data: θ̂⋆ from the

regression that includes the covariate, and π̂ from a simple auxiliary regression. The formula is an algebraic

identity of OLS that holds in cross-sectional, TWFE, and ADL models, for any treatment type. Combined

with a DAG, it tells the researcher whether the difference represents collider bias (exclude the covariate),

omitted variable bias (include it), or over-control of a mediator (include only its lagged value).

The second is a practical benchmark for the contemporaneous treatment effect (CET): use ADL + FE with

lagged state variables as the default specification. Include Zt−1 when the lagged covariate is substantively

appropriate (confounder or dual role). Avoid conditioning on contemporaneous Zt when it may be post-

treatment—either a collider or a mediator in the period of interest. This recommendation reinforces the

qualitative guidance of Blackwell and Glynn (2018) and Imai and Kim (2021); our contribution is the quan-

titative diagnostic formula and systematic Monte Carlo validation across 238 DGP configurations, which

confirm that this benchmark reduces collider bias to less than 3% of β, preserves the total contemporaneous

effect when covariates are lagged rather than contemporaneous, and is robust to bounded nonlinearities and

moderate outcome-to-treatment feedback. Across six published studies spanning democratization, peace-

keeping, fiscal capacity, infrastructure, and sovereign debt, the median |IVB/SE| among collider candidates

is approximately 0.13—well within sampling noise.


                                                      31
The temporal structure of TSCS data provides a structural advantage for this problem. In the canonical two-

period DID, a covariate that is both a confounder and a contemporaneous mediator presents an inescapable

dilemma: including it satisfies conditional parallel trends but blocks the indirect causal channel; excluding it

avoids over-control but leaves confounding unaddressed. In TSCS, the lagged value Zt−1 separates the two

roles: it absorbs confounding without blocking the contemporaneous path Dt → Zt → Yt . This is the timing

logic that the ADL benchmark exploits, and it connects to the active research on bad controls in the DID

literature (Caetano et al., 2022)—showing that in TSCS settings focused on the CET, a simpler solution

already exists.

This combination of a general diagnostic and a specific benchmark is the paper’s contribution. Without IVB,

the paper would risk collapsing into a familiar recommendation to use dynamic specifications. Without the

benchmark, IVB would risk remaining an algebraic curiosity. Put together, they provide both a language

for the problem and an operational default for a common applied setting. The extension to DiD with time-

varying controls (Section~6) shows that the same decomposition logic applies beyond the TSCS domain:

in DiD, it separates the safe pre-treatment adjustment from the potentially contaminated realized controls,

providing a disciplined hierarchy that the standard “with versus without controls’ ’ comparison does not

offer.

The limits are equally important. The benchmark is local to the CET; a lagged covariate that is pre-treatment

for Dt → Yt need not be harmless for distributed, cumulative, or long-run effects. Additional simulations

show that the benchmark remains robust to nonlinear effects of lagged controls and level-dependent trends,

but not to persistent unobserved contemporaneous confounding (Bellemare, Masaki and Pepinsky, 2017).

The IVB formula is derived for linear models; while bounded nonlinearities preserve its practical relevance,

the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem does not hold for nonlinear link functions. And the benchmark requires

T large enough for Nickell bias from Yt−1 to be negligible—in our simulations, T = 30 suffices, but in panels

with T ≤ 10 the researcher should consider GMM or other short-panel methods.

Two directions for future work are especially promising. First, just as Cinelli and Hazlett (2020) developed

sensitivity tools that extend OVB to unobserved confounders, a parallel development for IVB would allow

researchers to ask: “How strong would the collider relationship need to be to change my conclusions?’ ’

Second, extending the timing logic developed here to synthetic-control-style and interactive fixed-effects

estimators is an important agenda, given the growing use of these methods in comparative politics.

We provide the compute_ivb_multi() function as supplementary material, enabling researchers to compute

IVB decompositions for any TWFE or ADL specification with minimal effort. The combination of DAGs



                                                      32
for classifying covariates, the IVB formula for quantifying the stakes, and the ADL benchmark for specifica-

tion choice gives applied researchers a practical toolkit for the most consequential decision in observational

research: which variables to include as controls.

A.3     Appendix C: Interaction with Nickell Bias

In panels with unit fixed effects and short time dimensions, the OLS estimates of ADL coefficients suffer from

Nickell bias (Nickell, 1981), which arises because the within-transformation creates a mechanical correlation

between the demeaned lagged dependent variable ỹi,t−1 = yi,t−1 − ȳi and the demeaned error term ẽit =

eit − ēi . The bias is of order O(1/T ): it can be severe when T < 10 but becomes negligible as T grows

(Judson and Owen, 1999).

Because the IVB formula is an algebraic identity—it decomposes the finite-sample difference between two

nested OLS regressions—it remains exactly valid even in the presence of Nickell bias. The identity β̂ ⋆ − β̂ =

−θ̂⋆ × π̂ holds by construction. However, the causal interpretation of the components changes: in a panel with

short T , both θ̂⋆ and π̂ reflect a mixture of the true collider relationship and the dynamic bias introduced by

the within-transformation. The net effect on the IVB product is ambiguous in sign but bounded by O(1/T ),

and simulation evidence in Judson and Owen (1999) suggests it becomes negligible once T exceeds 20–30

periods. The direction of the IVB tends to be preserved even when magnitudes are contaminated: the sign

of −θ̂⋆ π̂ remains informative about the direction of collider bias. In short panels (T < 20), bias-corrected

estimators (Arellano and Bond, 1991) or the half-panel jackknife may be needed before giving the IVB

components a purely collider-bias interpretation.

A.6     Appendix F: IVB in the Potential Outcomes Framework

This appendix re-derives the IVB formula in the potential outcomes notation standard in the DID literature,

and connects it to the decomposition in Caetano and Callaway (2024).

Setup. Consider two periods: t∗ − 1 (pre-treatment) and t∗ (post-treatment). Treatment is binary: Di ∈

{0, 1} (no unit treated at t∗ − 1). Each unit has potential outcomes Yit (1), Yit (0) and potential covariates

Zit (1), Zit (0). The estimand is AT T = E[Yt∗ (1) − Yt∗ (0) | D = 1].

First-differenced TWFE. With two periods, the TWFE specification Yit = αi + λt + τ Dit + βZit + eit

reduces to:

                                        ∆Yit∗ = τ Di + β∆Zit∗ + ∆eit∗


where ∆ denotes first differences. The short model (without Z) gives τS .

IVB derivation. By FWL applied to this first-differenced regression:

                                                                  Cov(D, ∆Z)
                                   τ − τS = −β × π,          π=
                                                                    Var(D)

This is the IVB formula in potential outcomes notation. Both β and π are directly estimable. The component

π captures how much the treatment changes the covariate: if D does not affect Z (i.e., Zit∗ (1) = Zit∗ (0) for

all i), then π = 0 and including Z is harmless. When D affects Z, π ̸= 0 and the IVB is non-zero.

Connection to Caetano and Callaway (2024). Under their Assumption~3 (strict exogeneity of covari-

ates), D does not affect Z, so π = 0 and the IVB does not appear in their decomposition. Their Proposition~1

decomposes TWFE bias into terms (A)–(C) reflecting hidden linearity bias. When D does affect Z—violating

their Assumption~3, which is the “bad controls’ ’ case—the IVB adds to their decomposition:

                                              IVB (bad control bias)    hidden linearity bias

The IVB formula quantifies the first component; the diagnostics of Caetano and Callaway (2024) address

the second. The two are complementary.

Why TSCS has a structural advantage. In the two-period DID, the researcher has only Zt∗ (con-

temporaneous). If Z is both a confounder (parallel trends hold only conditional on Z) and a mediator

(D → Z → Y ), an inescapable dilemma arises: including Zt∗ satisfies conditional parallel trends but gen-

erates over-control; excluding it avoids over-control but violates conditional parallel trends. This is the

problem motivating the doubly robust and imputation-based methods of Caetano et al. (2022).

In TSCS (T > 2), the temporal structure resolves this dilemma. The ADL specification conditions on Zt−1 ,

which is pre-treatment for the contemporaneous effect of Dt on Yt . Under sequential ignorability (Black-

well and Glynn, 2018), conditioning on {Yt−1 , Zt−1 } blocks all backdoor paths—including those through

colliders—without blocking the contemporaneous mediator channel Dt → Zt → Yt . The lagged value ab-

sorbs confounding (satisfying conditional parallel trends) while the contemporaneous value is left out of

the regression, preserving the total effect. This separation is the timing logic that the ADL benchmark in

Section~5.4 exploits.

                           Var(IVB)

A.9         Appendix I: Control Classification and Full IVB Results
